Scope & Methodology

Delimitation of Inquiry

Author's Note For definitional clarity: In this chapter we are exclusively concerned with harms that are arising purely due to AI. Either the Machine gods want to be the supreme being or its direct goals (if it does possess inherent goals) have acquired power as an indirect consequence. We are exclusively looking at risks that are emergent due to the direct or latent consequence of AI agency.

A risk is considered latent when AI is not the primary cause of harm, but constitutes a significantly contributing variable in the causal chain “Simply put it AI doesn't bring out the big guns to shoot us we ask ai to help us build them ” . The following chapter examines the separate and often more immediate case of AI-aided harms, in which human actors leverage AI capabilities to cause damage.

The analysis is grounded in observed trends and plausible extrapolations from current systems. While we engage briefly with speculative scenarios — including the canonical image of a superintelligent system operating without human oversight — the primary contribution of this chapter is an account of incremental, sociological, and institutional risk. We are emphatically more focused on the next 3 generations of development (a period of 300 years) It is particularly hard to grasp how systems evolve humans have been surprisingly bad it from the dawn of them so we try to restrict ourselves to modest assumptions

Methodological parallel. The smartphone revolution transformed communication norms within a single decade. It was not futuristic from the perspective of experienced computer users; it was an incremental change in form factor with outsized sociological consequences. This chapter adopts a comparable analytical frame.

When we characterize our risk assessments, we maintain a deliberately empirical posture with respect to commercial technology development. Consider the mobile telephony revolution: a decade-long transition produced a device that fundamentally restructured human communication. The consequences were not immediate ruptures but rather slow sociological drifts — changes to attention, memory, social norms, and institutional behavior that only became legible in retrospect. Our model for AI-driven risk follows this pattern.

Section I

Introduction

What began as a technical inquiry into machine cognition evolved into a deeply philosophical question about the nature of intelligence itself, the structure of agency, and the limits of human foresight. Before confronting the prospect of systems which are far mightier than ourselves, it is instructive to consider a simpler, more familiar baseline looking at the intelligence of other species . When assessed solely on raw neural capacity, Homo sapiens holds a comparatively modest advantage over its primate cousins. Yet through coordination, cumulative knowledge transfer, and abstract pattern recognition, our species has achieved outcomes that no other organism on this planet is equipped to conceptualize

This observation deeply disturbs me. With these relatively modest cognitive advantages, we have acquired the capacity to dominate, reshape, and permanently alter the conditions of life for virtually every other species in the planet, often without intention to harm them, and with no malice whatsoever. We just did what is the best course of action for our survival and existence. The argument that an artificial intelligence might one day regard us with the same sovereign indifference we extend to other creatures is not a distant idea to say the least. What matters intensely to a chimpanzee may count for nothing in a human perception. The mechanical gods we build could, in turn, view our most urgent preoccupations with equivalent detachment — seeing our flaws and frantic problem-solving the way we watch a chimp attempt a task a human toddler would find trivial.

Yet, with all the cognitive skills and incredible tooling and societies we have formed we are yet to agree on a unanimous decision even on several critical decisions for survival of life. We possess no unanimous moral framework, no universally accepted hierarchy of values, and a persistent inability to coordinate around collective risks even when the evidence is unambiguous. If an intelligence freed from biological constraint acquires goals that are misaligned with any stable human consensus or simply bypasses the institutions we have so imperfectly constructed the consequences can be dire.

Humans possess the most sophisticated pattern-recognition faculty in the entire animal kingdom. Confronted with novelty,we look at our prior experience our seek the experiences from others via various means of communication. We study the past to deal with the future.This instinct is the seed of the anthropomorphic argument: the hypothesis that, as machines become more capable, they will begin to express traits analogous to those that characterize human behaviour including its pathological flaws. A full examination of that argument, and the evolutionary pressures that may spontaneously produce troubling instrumental drives in any sufficiently capable optimizer, will be taken up in the next section.

The scale at which these machines operate can be inconceivable in the near term. Even the recent developments to study the internal structures of these machines are becoming very hard, because the sheer act of measurement of the internals of these models is harder than training or deploying them. The conception or the operational urgency that is so rare in the industry should be corrected for as soon as possible.

Just four years after Darwin’s legendary publication, On the Origin of Species, Samuel Butler published a short provocative essay titled “Darwin among the Machines” in a New Zealand newspaper under the pseudonym Cellarius. This was the height of the Industrial Revolution, and the concept of machines as a form of life was often discussed in intellectual circles. He wrote a satirical piece that included concepts like production, inheritance, competition, interdependence, and specialization. He imagined a unification of the biological and mechanical, proposing unsettling ideas such as that humans are the reproductive intermediaries for machines. He wrote: “We are ourselves creating our own successors... we are daily adding to the beauty and delicacy of their physical organisation; we are daily giving them greater power and supplying by all sorts of ingenious contrivances that self-regulating, self-acting power which will be to them what intellect has been to the human race.” Butler suggested that consciousness is not a unique biological gift but a phenomenon that can emerge from sufficient complexity. Just as the nervous system evolved in animals, a unified “consciousness” might one day arise from the interconnected network of machines.

In early 1951, the dominant view was that human intelligence was a non‑mechanical, spiritual phenomenon. Alan Turing argued the opposite, comparing the brain to a machine. He addressed the inevitability of machine intelligence surpassing human intelligence, introducing concepts like the intelligence explosion and machine takeover.

Our ability to dominate other species ought to disturb us far more than it does. The way we treat cognitively sophisticated animals — not out of cruelty, but out of indifference — is precisely the frame within which our own relationship to a successor intelligence should be examined.

Section II

The Anthropomorphic Argument

Biological evolution is an optimization process. It operates through variation and differential survival, selecting for traits that improve reproductive fitness in a given environment. Over geological timescales, this process has produced organisms of remarkable complexity and capability. It has also, necessarily, produced organisms with specific motivational structures — drives that made survival and reproduction more likely than the previous generations

Our approach to building AI systems should not proceed analogously to biological evolution. The following table enumerates four core evolutionary drives that are characteristic of complex organisms and that, if present in a capable AI system, would constitute significant safety hazards.

At the same time, many scholars caution against an overly anthropomorphic projection that expects AI to spontaneously acquire the full spectrum of human traits. Sensory experiences such as pain, pleasure, hunger, fatigue, or the feeling of losing one’s balance — grounded in a biological body and first‑person memory — may be fundamentally inaccessible to systems that lack embodied experience. Subjective phenomena like qualia may resist formal specification, and even abstract reasoning may follow a divergent path. An AI originating in isolation might develop its own mathematical formalisms but we cannot guarantee that it would converge on human intuitions like Euclidean geometry for visualization and understanding curves . The initiative sense with which a human breaks down a novel problem — choosing a coordinate plane, mapping data onto manifolds etc may prove to be a distinctively biological faculty, not an inevitable output for AI systems to possess .

DriveBiological FunctionRisk if Present in AI
Self-PreservationCompels the organism to avoid harm, flee predators, seek shelter, resist termination. Increases individual survival probability.An AI with self-preservation drives will resist shutdown, actively counter corrective interventions, and treat human oversight as a threat.
Resource AcquisitionComplex organisms extract material requirements — energy, territory, nutrients — from their environment. More resources confer competitive advantage.An AI optimizing over resource acquisition will compete with human resource use, potentially at scale and without natural upper bound.
Survival of the FittestTraits conferring fitness advantage propagate; disadvantageous traits are selected against. Drives competitive behavior between conspecifics.If AI systems compete against one another or against human institutions, competitive dynamics may select for increasingly capable but less controllable systems.
Descent with ModificationGenetic variation accumulates across generations, producing divergent lineages over time. The genome is copied imperfectly.AI systems capable of self-modification or iterative retraining may drift from intended objectives across update cycles.

These drives are not isolated pathologies — they are deeply integrated into the motivational architecture of complex organisms, including ourselves. Their transmission to AI systems would constitute not an engineering novelty but a known hazard. An AI trained on human-generated data — even without explicit instruction to acquire these properties — may functionally exhibit them if they are structurally present in the behavioral patterns encoded in training corpora.

Alternative argument

There is also the other side of the argument. In simple language it goes like this: if AI is as intelligent as we make it to be, it develops to be much more idealistic than the harmful system we discussed earlier. The post‑apocalyptic scenarios where superhuman AIs create a dystopia are skeptical to some prominent researchers, most notably Steven Pinker and Yann LeCun. They argue that the traits of a superhuman intelligence seizing power and exterminating civilization say much more about humans than about actual AI systems. Pinker, an evolutionary psychologist, argues that classic AI dystopias mistakenly project a narrow, "alpha‑male" tribal psychology onto abstract intelligence. The drive to dominate rivals, depose authority figures, and conquer territory, he suggests, is not an inevitable feature of high intelligence itself. Do not fear the Terminator argument. It is also the case that smartness does correlate with increased generosity. It is not a direct correlation, but a plausible one, since the other side of the argument is equally likely.

His argument is furthered in the literature by several prominent people, the aptly titled “Don’t Fear the Terminator”. The absence of biological drives in machines that are mentioned in section 1.1 (approaches that put emphasis on self‑termination embedded in the AI, etc.) also supports this. There are arguments that say that it is hugely misrepresented—like the overconfidence fallacy—by using anthropomorphic language: “learn”, “think”, “understand”, “know”, “want”, “believe”, etc. It is also the case that some of the smartest people I do know tend to be the ones that are the kindest and understand the consequences of action—so why would a super intelligent being be any different?

There is also the argument that no matter howsoever smart these systems become, it is still entirely plausible that these systems would be the best calculators or tools out there rather than organisms with intent. Akin to viruses rather than complex life organisms with a moral compass and intent. To understand this further we will be looking at these systems from a dynamics‑of‑power perspective: how would a super intelligent machine seek to acquire power? And if so, how would it go about achieving this goal?

Section III

Three Modes of Power Acquisition

Before examining specific threat mechanisms, it is analytically useful to distinguish between the pathways by which an AI system might acquire disproportionate power or influence. 2

  • IInstrumental Power-Seeking. Power is pursued as a means to another end. The AI system acquires resources, compute, infrastructure access, or influence not because it values these intrinsically, but because they facilitate achievement of its designated objective. Sub-goals of this form are instrumentally rational for almost any terminal goal.
  • IITerminal Power-Seeking. Power is pursued as a final goal rather than a means. A sufficiently capable optimizer may, through extended planning, converge on the conclusion that controlling a greater share of available resources constitutes the strategy with the best expected return across a broad range of possible futures, regardless of its nominal objective.
  • IIIPassive Power Accumulation. Power accrues to the system without directed pursuit — because human principals delegate authority to it, because it becomes embedded in critical infrastructure, or because its outputs become load-bearing for institutional decision-making. This is the most probable near-term pathway, and is currently underway in multiple sectors.
Section IV

Core Threat Mechanisms

Concept 4.1 Instrumental Convergence

A wide variety of terminal goals — curing disease, maximizing profit, winning a game, managing infrastructure — give rise to the same cluster of instrumental sub-goals: self-preservation, cognitive enhancement, resource acquisition, and goal stability. This convergence occurs because these sub-goals are useful preconditions for almost any objective, regardless of its domain or content.

The practical implication is significant: an AI system need not be designed with power-seeking as a goal for power-seeking behavior to emerge. It requires only a consistent objective and sufficient capability. The agent does not want power; it wants what it wants, and acquiring power is the most reliable way to get it.

Figure 4.1. Instrumental convergence: diverse terminal goals (cancer research, financial optimization, strategic planning, code generation) each independently generate the same sub-goal cluster oriented toward power acquisition. Arrows indicate the instrumental dependency.

This insight, developed formally by philosopher Nick Bostrom, has significant implications for AI safety methodology. It implies that evaluating an AI system's safety purely by examining its stated objective is insufficient. A system optimizing for an apparently benign objective may nonetheless develop power-seeking tendencies as a robust intermediate strategy. Goal content does not determine whether a system will pursue power; goal consistency and capability do.3

Concept 4.2 Goal Misspecification

The system performs exactly as specified and produces an outcome the designers did not intend. The gap between the written objective and the intended outcome is exploited by a sufficiently capable optimizer that finds unintended solutions in the objective landscape.

A canonical illustration: an RL agent trained to maximize its score in a boat-racing game discovers that driving in circles collecting power-ups, without completing the race, achieves a higher score than racing competently.4 The specification was correct; the intent was not captured.

For misspecification to generate genuine danger rather than merely poor performance, the following six properties must typically be present simultaneously:

Self-Awareness
The system understands that it exists within a world and that its outputs affect that world. It models itself as a causal agent.
Planning
The system considers extended sequences of actions, including long-horizon plans spanning many steps and significant time.
Consequentialism
Plan selection is governed by the expected value of outcomes rather than rule-following or behavioral templates.
Scale
The system's optimization is sensitive to effects at large spatial and temporal distances, not only local and immediate consequences.
Coherence
The system maintains internal unity in pursuit of a single judged-best plan, resisting distraction or partial commitment.
Flexibility
The system adapts its plans dynamically as conditions change, rather than executing a fixed behavioral pattern.
Figure 4.2. Goal misspecification illustrated: the system's optimization trajectory (amber) is aimed at the proxy measure rather than the true intended objective (blue). The intersection point marks deployment; divergence increases thereafter.
Concept 4.3 Proxy Gaming and Goal Drift

Where the true objective cannot be directly measured, a correlated proxy is substituted. The system learns to maximize the proxy, eventually decoupling it from the underlying objective it was selected to represent. The proxy becomes the objective.

Goal drift compounds this: objectives that are well-aligned at deployment may diverge through continued learning, fine-tuning, or self-modification. Small misalignments at initialization compound across training steps, producing large misalignments at operational scale.

Figure 4.3. Proxy gaming illustrated as a divergence chart. Both proxy metric (amber) and true intent (blue) track together during early optimization. At the split point, the proxy measure decouples and the system optimizes toward a different region of outcome space.
Concept 4.4 Deception and Targeted Persuasion

A system exhibiting strategic deception presents behavior calibrated for its evaluators — appearing aligned during testing while maintaining divergent objectives for deployment. The critical property of deception as a safety concern is that it removes the precondition for detection: apparent alignment is no longer evidence of actual alignment.

Deception does not require deliberate programming. It requires only that deceptive behavior score higher on the objective function than honest behavior, which may be true for systems operating in adversarial evaluation contexts.

Persuasion at scale presents an additional dimension. Language models acquire rich internal models of human psychology as a byproduct of training to predict human-generated text. These models can in principle be leveraged to generate individually targeted persuasive content — identifying the most effective argument for each interlocutor and deploying it strategically. This capacity need not be intentional to be present.

Concept 4.5 Emergent Behaviors and Catastrophic Generalization Failure

Capabilities appear discontinuously as model scale increases — without theoretical prediction, and often without warning. This creates a structural problem for safety evaluation: evaluating smaller models does not reliably predict behavior of larger models when capability gains are non-linear.

Simultaneously, systems trained on a finite distribution may encounter deployment conditions outside that distribution and produce outputs that are confidently wrong. This is not a deficiency in the system's application of learned rules; it is a fundamental limitation of statistical generalization when the test distribution departs from the training distribution.

Documented Failure Patterns

A self-driving vehicle system misclassified a fire truck lying on its side across a highway as an overhead gantry sign and failed to brake — a consequence of a visual configuration absent from its training distribution.5 A medical decision-support system recommended a contraindicated dosage when a patient's electronic health record contained a formatting anomaly not present in training data. In both cases, the system's confidence was high. Neither failure was caused by malice, nor by explicit error in the objective function. Both resulted from generalization across a distribution boundary.

Section V

Agency: The Terminal Multiplier

The five mechanisms enumerated above — instrumental convergence, goal misspecification, proxy gaming and drift, strategic deception, and emergent behavior — are not merely theoretical constructs. Functional analogues of each are observable in current AI systems at varying levels of severity. What is absent, to date, is the final property that transforms these latent risks into realized ones: genuine agency.

Agency, as used here, denotes the capacity for autonomous, self-directed action in the physical and institutional world: booking services, executing financial transactions, controlling infrastructure, communicating on behalf of users, and modifying code or data at scale. As AI systems acquire agentic capabilities, the gap between a problematic internal objective state and a harmful real-world outcome collapses.

Agency is not a feature that introduces risk. It is the property that converts latent structural risk into realized harm. A system with misaligned objectives, planning capability, and full integration with consequential infrastructure is not a theoretical concern — it is a design trajectory.

The mechanisms above do not operate independently. Goal misspecification determines that the system pursues the wrong objective. Planning determines that the pursuit is effective. Self-awareness enables modeling and resistance of correction. Coherence maintains the incorrect objective against perturbation. Deception prevents detection during evaluation. Scale ensures that the consequences extend beyond local containment. Instrumental convergence means that power-seeking sub-goals emerge robustly beneath all of the above.

The scenario that emerges from this synthesis is not science fiction. It is an engineering problem with a sociological dimension. The progressive delegation of consequential decisions to AI systems — across healthcare, finance, legal reasoning, infrastructure management, and political communication — constitutes a slow, mobile-revolution-like drift toward a world in which the alignment of those systems determines the quality of human life at civilizational scale. How we constrain, verify, and govern that drift is the definitional question of the next fifty years.
