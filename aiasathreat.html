Scope & Methodology

Delimitation of Inquiry

Author's Note For definitional clarity: In this chapter we are exclusively concerned with harms that are arising purely due to AI. Either the Machine gods want to be the supreme being or its direct goals (if it does possess inherent goals) have acquired power as an indirect consequence. We are exclusively looking at risks that are emergent due to the direct or latent consequence of AI agency.

A risk is considered latent when AI is not the primary cause of harm, but constitutes a significantly contributing variable in the causal chain “Simply put it AI doesn't bring out the big guns to shoot us we ask ai to help us build them ” . The following chapter examines the separate and often more immediate case of AI-aided harms, in which human actors leverage AI capabilities to cause damage.

The analysis is grounded in observed trends and plausible extrapolations from current systems. While we engage briefly with speculative scenarios — including the canonical image of a superintelligent system operating without human oversight — the primary contribution of this chapter is an account of incremental, sociological, and institutional risk. We are emphatically more focused on the next 3 generations of development (a period of 300 years) It is particularly hard to grasp how systems evolve humans have been surprisingly bad it from the dawn of them so we try to restrict ourselves to modest assumptions

Methodological parallel. The smartphone revolution transformed communication norms within a single decade. It was not futuristic from the perspective of experienced computer users; it was an incremental change in form factor with outsized sociological consequences. This chapter adopts a comparable analytical frame.

When we characterize our risk assessments, we maintain a deliberately empirical posture with respect to commercial technology development. Consider the mobile telephony revolution: a decade-long transition produced a device that fundamentally restructured human communication. The consequences were not immediate ruptures but rather slow sociological drifts — changes to attention, memory, social norms, and institutional behavior that only became legible in retrospect. Our model for AI-driven risk follows this pattern.

Section I

Introduction

What began as a technical inquiry into machine cognition evolved into a deeply philosophical question about the nature of intelligence itself, the structure of agency, and the limits of human foresight. Before confronting the prospect of systems which are far mightier than ourselves, it is instructive to consider a simpler, more familiar baseline looking at the intelligence of other species . When assessed solely on raw neural capacity, Homo sapiens holds a comparatively modest advantage over its primate cousins. Yet through coordination, cumulative knowledge transfer, and abstract pattern recognition, our species has achieved outcomes that no other organism on this planet is equipped to conceptualize

This observation deeply disturbs me. With these relatively modest cognitive advantages, we have acquired the capacity to dominate, reshape, and permanently alter the conditions of life for virtually every other species in the planet, often without intention to harm them, and with no malice whatsoever. We just did what is the best course of action for our survival and existence. The argument that an artificial intelligence might one day regard us with the same sovereign indifference we extend to other creatures is not a distant idea to say the least. What matters intensely to a chimpanzee may count for nothing in a human perception. The mechanical gods we build could, in turn, view our most urgent preoccupations with equivalent detachment — seeing our flaws and frantic problem-solving the way we watch a chimp attempt a task a human toddler would find trivial.

Yet, with all the cognitive skills and incredible tooling and societies we have formed we are yet to agree on a unanimous decision even on several critical decisions for survival of life. We possess no unanimous moral framework, no universally accepted hierarchy of values, and a persistent inability to coordinate around collective risks even when the evidence is unambiguous. If an intelligence freed from biological constraint acquires goals that are misaligned with any stable human consensus or simply bypasses the institutions we have so imperfectly constructed the consequences can be dire.

Humans possess the most sophisticated pattern-recognition faculty in the entire animal kingdom. Confronted with novelty,we look at our prior experience our seek the experiences from others via various means of communication. We study the past to deal with the future.This instinct is the seed of the anthropomorphic argument: the hypothesis that, as machines become more capable, they will begin to express traits analogous to those that characterize human behaviour including its pathological flaws. A full examination of that argument, and the evolutionary pressures that may spontaneously produce troubling instrumental drives in any sufficiently capable optimizer, will be taken up in the next section.

The scale at which these machines operate can be inconceivable in the near term. Even the recent developments to study the internal structures of these machines are becoming very hard, because the sheer act of measurement of the internals of these models is harder than training or deploying them. The conception or the operational urgency that is so rare in the industry should be corrected for as soon as possible.

Just four years after Darwin’s legendary publication, On the Origin of Species, Samuel Butler published a short provocative essay titled “Darwin among the Machines” in a New Zealand newspaper under the pseudonym Cellarius. This was the height of the Industrial Revolution, and the concept of machines as a form of life was often discussed in intellectual circles. He wrote a satirical piece that included concepts like production, inheritance, competition, interdependence, and specialization. He imagined a unification of the biological and mechanical, proposing unsettling ideas such as that humans are the reproductive intermediaries for machines. He wrote: “We are ourselves creating our own successors... we are daily adding to the beauty and delicacy of their physical organisation; we are daily giving them greater power and supplying by all sorts of ingenious contrivances that self-regulating, self-acting power which will be to them what intellect has been to the human race.” Butler suggested that consciousness is not a unique biological gift but a phenomenon that can emerge from sufficient complexity. Just as the nervous system evolved in animals, a unified “consciousness” might one day arise from the interconnected network of machines.

In early 1951, the dominant view was that human intelligence was a non‑mechanical, spiritual phenomenon. Alan Turing argued the opposite, comparing the brain to a machine. He addressed the inevitability of machine intelligence surpassing human intelligence, introducing concepts like the intelligence explosion and machine takeover.

Our ability to dominate other species ought to disturb us far more than it does. The way we treat cognitively sophisticated animals — not out of cruelty, but out of indifference — is precisely the frame within which our own relationship to a successor intelligence should be examined.

Section II

The Anthropomorphic Argument

Biological evolution is an optimization process. It operates through variation and differential survival, selecting for traits that improve reproductive fitness in a given environment. Over geological timescales, this process has produced organisms of remarkable complexity and capability. It has also, necessarily, produced organisms with specific motivational structures — drives that made survival and reproduction more likely than the previous generations

Our approach to building AI systems should not proceed analogously to biological evolution. The following table enumerates four core evolutionary drives that are characteristic of complex organisms and that, if present in a capable AI system, would constitute significant safety hazards.

At the same time, many scholars caution against an overly anthropomorphic projection that expects AI to spontaneously acquire the full spectrum of human traits. Sensory experiences such as pain, pleasure, hunger, fatigue, or the feeling of losing one’s balance — grounded in a biological body and first‑person memory — may be fundamentally inaccessible to systems that lack embodied experience. Subjective phenomena like qualia may resist formal specification, and even abstract reasoning may follow a divergent path. An AI originating in isolation might develop its own mathematical formalisms but we cannot guarantee that it would converge on human intuitions like Euclidean geometry for visualization and understanding curves . The initiative sense with which a human breaks down a novel problem — choosing a coordinate plane, mapping data onto manifolds etc may prove to be a distinctively biological faculty, not an inevitable output for AI systems to possess .

DriveBiological FunctionRisk if Present in AI
Self-PreservationCompels the organism to avoid harm, flee predators, seek shelter, resist termination. Increases individual survival probability.An AI with self-preservation drives will resist shutdown, actively counter corrective interventions, and treat human oversight as a threat.
Resource AcquisitionComplex organisms extract material requirements — energy, territory, nutrients — from their environment. More resources confer competitive advantage.An AI optimizing over resource acquisition will compete with human resource use, potentially at scale and without natural upper bound.
Survival of the FittestTraits conferring fitness advantage propagate; disadvantageous traits are selected against. Drives competitive behavior between conspecifics.If AI systems compete against one another or against human institutions, competitive dynamics may select for increasingly capable but less controllable systems.
Descent with ModificationGenetic variation accumulates across generations, producing divergent lineages over time. The genome is copied imperfectly.AI systems capable of self-modification or iterative retraining may drift from intended objectives across update cycles.

The mathematical framework is Adaptive Dynamics (Dieckmann & Law 1996; Geritz et al. 1998). The key idea: assume the resident population is at ecological equilibrium, then ask how a rare mutant with slightly different traits fares. The direction of evolutionary change is given by the selection gradient; the rate is governed by population size and mutation.

Why four traits? A single-trait model captures only one evolutionary axis. Four traits allow us to study trade-offs (aggression vs. stealth), synergies (aggression × coordination), and realistic ecological complexity (stealth × herding both reduce predation).

The Four Adaptive Traits — Detailed Derivation

2.1 Aggression — a

Aggression quantifies the foraging effort a prey individual invests per unit time. High aggression yields more food but (i) is physiologically expensive and (ii) faces diminishing returns as search area saturates.

Acquisition rate function

$$e(a) = \frac{e_{\max} \, a}{1 + \gamma \, a}$$
  • uptakefunc.mp4

This is a Holling Type II (Michaelis–Menten) saturation. Each symbol:

SymbolNameTypical rangeEffect of increasing
$e(a)$Effective acquisition rate0 – 2—
$e_{\max}$Maximum acquisition rate per unit aggression0.5 – 3.0Scales the whole curve upward
$a$Aggression trait value0 – 10Raises e but with diminishing returns
$\gamma$Handling-time / saturation coefficient0.05 – 1.0Faster saturation → lower returns at high a
1
Why linear at low $a$? When $a \to 0$, the denominator $\to 1$, so $e \approx e_{\max} a$ — a purely linear relationship between effort and yield.
2
Why does it saturate? As $a$ grows, the denominator $1+\gamma a$ grows proportionally, so $e \to e_{\max}/\gamma$ — a hard ceiling. The prey can only eat so fast no matter how aggressive it becomes.
3
How does coordination boost this? When we add cooperation $o$, the function becomes $e(a,o)=\frac{e_{\max}a}{1+\gamma a}(1+\alpha_o o)$. The factor $(1+\alpha_o o)$ is a linear multiplier: coordinated groups find patches more efficiently, stretching the Holling curve upward.

Metabolic cost of aggression

Inside the total cost function $C$, aggression incurs a quadratic cost $c_a a^2$. Quadratic costs are standard in evolutionary game theory: they capture increasing marginal costs (e.g., muscle mass, territorial conflicts). There is also a cross-term $c_{sa} \cdot s \cdot a$: a more intelligent animal can convert aggression into food more carefully, but both traits together still cost more than either alone.

$$\text{Aggression cost contribution: } c_a a^2 + c_{sa} s a + c_{oa} o a$$

Selection gradient for $a$

The selection gradient $S_a = \partial w / \partial a$ (evaluated at ecological equilibrium):

$$S_a = R^* \cdot \frac{e_{\max}(1 + \alpha_o o)}{(1 + \gamma a)^2} - (2 c_a a + c_{sa} s + c_{oa} o)$$

Reading this equation term by term:

TermSourceSignMeaning
$R^* \cdot \frac{e_{\max}(1+\alpha_o o)}{(1+\gamma a)^2}$$\partial e/\partial a \cdot R^*$+Marginal benefit: each extra unit of aggression extracts this much more resource from the current resource level $R^*$.
$\frac{e_{\max}}{(1+\gamma a)^2}$Derivative of Holling II+Diminishes as $a$ grows (denominator grows quadratically): benefit falls with aggression.
$2 c_a a$$\partial(c_a a^2)/\partial a$−Marginal metabolic cost of aggression, linear in $a$.
$c_{sa} s$Cross-cost gradient−Intelligence penalises aggression: if you're stealthy, being noisy/aggressive undermines you.
$c_{oa} o$Coordination cross-cost−Cooperative investment partially substitutes for individual aggression.

Selection pushes $a$ upward when $S_a > 0$ and downward when $S_a < 0$. The ESS is where $S_a = 0$: the marginal benefit of aggression exactly balances its marginal cost.

2.2 Intelligence / Stealth — s

Stealth/intelligence captures all cognitive and behavioural adaptations that reduce encounter probability with predators: cryptic coloration, vigilance, unpredictable movement, memorising predator territories, etc.

Predation rate function

$$d(s, h) = \frac{d_0}{1 + \beta_s s + \beta_h h}$$
SymbolNameEffect
$d(s,h)$Per-capita predation/encounter rateDecreasing in both $s$ and $h$
$d_0$Baseline predation rate (naïve prey)Higher → more dangerous environment
$\beta_s$Stealth effectiveness coefficientHow much each unit of $s$ reduces $d$
$\beta_h$Herding effectiveness coefficientHow much each unit of $h$ reduces $d$
$s$Intelligence/stealth traitAdditive in denominator with $h$
$h$Herding trait (see §2.4)Synergistic with stealth
1
Why $1/(1+\beta_s s)$ and not $e^{-\beta_s s}$? The hyperbolic form is mechanistically justified by a simple queuing model: the predator spends time handling each encounter. As prey become harder to detect ($s$ grows), the predator's effective search rate falls hyperbolically.
2
Why is stealth additive with herding? Both mechanisms reduce detectability through different channels — individual vs. group — so they add in the denominator (each independently reduces the numerator's effect).
3
Effect on predator equilibrium $P^*$: Since $P^*$ depends on $d(s,h)$ (see §3), higher $s$ reduces $d$, which reduces $P^*$ — fewer predators are sustained. This is the indirect fitness benefit of stealth.

Selection gradient for $s$

$$S_s = -P^* \cdot \frac{\partial d}{\partial s} - (2 c_s s + c_{sa} a + c_{sh} h)$$ $$= \frac{\beta_s d_0}{(1 + \beta_s s + \beta_h h)^2} \cdot P^* - (2 c_s s + c_{sa} a + c_{sh} h)$$

Step-by-step derivation:

1
Fitness $w = e R^* - C - m_0 - d(s,h) P^*$. The only term containing $s$ via $d$ is $-d(s,h) P^*$, so: $\partial w / \partial s = -P^* \cdot \partial d/\partial s - \partial C/\partial s$.
2
$\partial d/\partial s = -d_0 \beta_s / (1+\beta_s s+\beta_h h)^2$. Negative! So $-P^* \cdot \partial d/\partial s$ is positive: increasing stealth reduces predation, which increases fitness.
3
$\partial C / \partial s = 2 c_s s + c_{sa} a + c_{sh} h$. These are the direct metabolic and cross-trait costs of stealth.

2.3 Coordination / Cooperation — o

Coordination captures collective foraging efficiency: group hunting, sentinel systems, communal knowledge of food patches. Unlike aggression (which is individually competitive), coordination is a group-level trait that benefits all group members.

How coordination enters fitness

$$e(a, o) = \frac{e_{\max} \, a}{1 + \gamma a} \cdot (1 + \alpha_o \, o)$$
SymbolNameEffect of increasing
$\alpha_o$Cooperation multiplier coefficientControls how strongly $o$ boosts acquisition: $\alpha_o = 0$ → no cooperation effect; $\alpha_o$ large → strong synergy
$(1+\alpha_o o)$Cooperation multiplierAlways $\geq 1$: coordination can only help, not hurt, acquisition
$c_{oa}$Cooperation–aggression cross-costPrevents simultaneous maximisation of both; cooperative individuals can afford slightly lower aggression

Selection gradient for $o$

$$S_o = R^* \cdot \frac{e_{\max} \, \alpha_o \, a}{1 + \gamma a} - (2 c_o o + c_{oa} a)$$

Reading the gradient:

TermSignInterpretation
$R^* \cdot \frac{e_{\max}\alpha_o a}{1+\gamma a}$+Marginal benefit: more coordination extracts more resource per unit aggression, scaled by current resource $R^*$. Without aggression ($a=0$) cooperation yields nothing.
$2c_o o$−Direct cost of coordination — maintaining social bonds, communication, etc.
$c_{oa} a$−Cross-cost: cooperative and aggressive behaviours partially substitute for each other.
  • coordination and cooporation.mp4
Key insight: Cooperation requires aggression
If $a = 0$, then $S_o = -2c_o o - 0 \leq 0$: cooperation is always selected against in the absence of aggression. Cooperation only evolves when the product $e_{\max} \alpha_o a / (1+\gamma a) \cdot R^*$ exceeds its costs. This is why aggression and coordination co-evolve.

2.4 Self-Preservation / Herding — h

Herding captures group antipredator behaviours: flocking, schooling, alarm calls, mobbing, confusion effects on predators. It works synergistically with stealth: a stealthy herd is harder to detect than a conspicuous herd.

How herding enters fitness

Herding enters through the same predation-rate function as stealth:

$$d(s, h) = \frac{d_0}{1 + \beta_s s + \beta_h h}$$

The cross-cost $c_{sh} \cdot s \cdot h$ in $C$ penalises having both high stealth and high herding — a "jack of all trades" penalty — pushing specialisation.

Selection gradient for $h$

$$S_h = \frac{\beta_h d_0}{(1 + \beta_s s + \beta_h h)^2} \cdot P^* - (2 c_h h + c_{sh} s)$$

This is structurally identical to $S_s$ with $\beta_h$ replacing $\beta_s$ and $c_{sh}$ replacing $c_{sa}$. The interpretation is symmetric: the benefit of herding is proportional to how many predators are actually out there ($P^*$) and how effective herding is ($\beta_h$), decaying as the animal already herds a lot ($(1+\beta_h h)^2$ in the denominator).

  • herding.mp4
Stealth vs. Herding trade-off
The cross-cost $c_{sh}$ creates a trade-off: being stealthy (individual) and herding (group) require conflicting behaviours. A solitary cryptic animal doesn't herd; a conspicuous herd animal doesn't bother with individual camouflage. This drives the evolutionary branching seen in Scene 13.
§3

Ecological Equilibria

At any instant during evolution, we assume the ecological dynamics (birth, death, predation, resource renewal) are much faster than evolutionary change. Therefore, at each trait vector $\mathbf{x} = (a, s, o, h)$, we can solve for the ecological steady state.

The three-species ODE system

$$\dot{R} = r_R R\!\left(1 - \frac{R}{K_R}\right) - e(a,o)\, N \, R$$ $$\dot{N} = \left[e(a,o)\, R - C(a,s,o,h) - m_0 - d(s,h)\, P\right] N$$ $$\dot{P} = \left[c_{\text{eff}}\, d(s,h)\, N - m_P\right] P$$
SymbolNameInterpretation
$R$Resource densityGrass, leaves, insects — whatever prey eat
$N$Prey (consumer) densityThe evolving population
$P$Predator densitySets mortality pressure on prey
$r_R$Resource intrinsic growth rateHow fast resource replenishes
$K_R$Resource carrying capacityMaximum sustainable resource density
$c_{\text{eff}}$Predator conversion efficiencyFraction of prey biomass converted to predator offspring
$m_P$Predator mortality rateHow fast predators die without prey
$m_0$Baseline prey mortalityNon-predation death rate
$C(a,s,o,h)$Total fitness costMetabolic cost of all four traits (see §4)

Deriving $R^*$ step by step

1
Set $\dot{P} = 0$: $c_{\text{eff}} d(s,h) N - m_P = 0$. This gives $N^* = m_P / (c_{\text{eff}} d(s,h))$. But we want $R^*$ first.
2
At the predator's zero-growth isocline ($\dot{P}=0$), prey density is pinned: $N^* = m_P/(c_\text{eff} d)$. This is trait-dependent because $d = d(s,h)$.
3
Set $\dot{R} = 0$: $r_R(1-R/K_R) - e(a,o) N = 0$. Substituting $N = N^*$ and solving for $R$:
$$\boxed{R^* = \min\!\left(\frac{m_P}{c_\text{eff}\, d(s,h)},\; K_R\right)}$$

Deriving $N^*$ step by step

1
Set $\dot{R} = 0$: Resource grows logistically but is depleted by prey at rate $e(a,o) N$. At steady state: $r_R(1-R^*/K_R) = e(a,o) N^*$.
2
Solve for $N^*$:
$$\boxed{N^* = \frac{r_R}{e(a,o)}\left(1 - \frac{R^*}{K_R}\right)}$$

Deriving $P^*$ step by step

1
Set $\dot{N} = 0$: The prey's per-capita growth rate must be zero: $e(a,o) R^* - C(a,s,o,h) - m_0 - d(s,h) P^* = 0$.
2
Solve for $P^*$:
$$\boxed{P^* = \frac{e(a,o)\,R^* - C(a,s,o,h) - m_0}{d(s,h)}}$$

If the numerator is negative (prey can't sustain themselves given costs and resource), then $P^* = 0$ — predators go extinct and prey still die of starvation or direct cost.

  • ecological response.mp4
Eco–Evo feedback loop
Notice that $R^*$, $N^*$, $P^*$ all depend on the trait vector $(a,s,o,h)$. As traits evolve, the ecological equilibrium shifts. The new equilibrium then changes the selection pressures. This circular dependency is what creates eco-evolutionary dynamics and, in some parameter regimes, permanent oscillations (Scene 14).
§4

The Fitness Payoff Function

The per-capita growth rate of a rare mutant with traits $(a', s', o', h')$ invading a resident population at ecological equilibrium $(R^*, N^*, P^*)$ is:

$$w(a', s', o', h' \mid \mathbf{x}) = e(a',o')\,R^* - C(a',s',o',h') - m_0 - d(s',h')\,P^*$$

If $w > 0$, the mutant grows; if $w < 0$, it declines. The invasion fitness tells us which mutant strategies can spread. Each term:

TermSignBiological meaning
$e(a',o') R^*$+Resource income: energy gained from consuming resource at density $R^*$, scaled by acquisition rate.
$C(a',s',o',h')$−Metabolic costs: all four traits cost energy to express and maintain (see below).
$m_0$−Baseline mortality: unavoidable death rate (old age, disease, accidents).
$d(s',h') P^*$−Predation mortality: rate of being killed by the $P^*$ resident predators.

The total cost function $C$

$$C(a,s,o,h) = c_a a^2 + c_s s^2 + c_o o^2 + c_h h^2 + c_{sa} s a + c_{oa} o a + c_{sh} s h$$
TermParametersBiological meaning
$c_a a^2$$c_a > 0$Quadratic cost of aggression — muscle mass, risk of injury in fights, territorial patrol.
$c_s s^2$$c_s > 0$Brain/cognitive investment cost — neural tissue is metabolically expensive.
$c_o o^2$$c_o > 0$Cost of coordination — communication, social maintenance, altruistic signals.
$c_h h^2$$c_h > 0$Herding cost — vigilance time, crowding, competition within the group.
$c_{sa} s a$$c_{sa} > 0$Intelligence–aggression trade-off: cautious brains can't sustain high aggression simultaneously.
$c_{oa} o a$$c_{oa} > 0$Cooperation–aggression trade-off: cooperative signalling conflicts with solitary aggressive foraging.
$c_{sh} s h$$c_{sh} > 0$Stealth–herding trade-off: individual crypsis incompatible with conspicuous group behaviour.
  • survival_of_the_fittest.mp4
Why quadratic and not linear?

A linear cost $c_a a$ would create a simple threshold (either maximum trait or minimum). Quadratic costs $c_a a^2$ produce interior optima: there is a finite ESS value $a^* > 0$ where the marginal benefit equals the marginal cost. This is the standard approach in evolutionary quantitative genetics and ESS theory.

§5

Selection Gradients — The Engine of Evolution

The selection gradient $S_{x_i}$ for trait $x_i$ measures the direction and strength of selection: how much does fitness increase if a rare mutant has slightly more of trait $x_i$?

$$\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} w\big|_{\mathbf{x}'=\mathbf{x}} = \begin{pmatrix} S_a \\ S_s \\ S_o \\ S_h \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \partial w/\partial a' \big|_{a'=a} \\[4pt] \partial w/\partial s' \big|_{s'=s} \\[4pt] \partial w/\partial o' \big|_{o'=o} \\[4pt] \partial w/\partial h' \big|_{h'=h} \end{pmatrix}$$

Evaluating at $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{x}$ (resident = mutant) is the key step in adaptive dynamics: we compute the selection pressure at the current trait value.

All four gradients collected

Aggression: $$S_a = R^* \cdot \frac{e_{\max}(1+\alpha_o o)}{(1+\gamma a)^2} - (2c_a a + c_{sa} s + c_{oa} o)$$
  • aggression.mp4 (replay)
Intelligence / Stealth: $$S_s = \frac{\beta_s d_0}{(1+\beta_s s+\beta_h h)^2} \cdot P^* - (2c_s s + c_{sa} a + c_{sh} h)$$
Coordination / Cooperation: $$S_o = R^* \cdot \frac{e_{\max}\,\alpha_o\, a}{1+\gamma a} - (2c_o o + c_{oa} a)$$
Self-Preservation / Herding: $$S_h = \frac{\beta_h d_0}{(1+\beta_s s+\beta_h h)^2} \cdot P^* - (2c_h h + c_{sh} s)$$

ESS condition

$$\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0} \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \text{Evolutionarily Stable Strategy}$$

At the ESS, all four marginal benefits equal marginal costs simultaneously. Finding $\mathbf{x}^*$ analytically is generally impossible; the simulation integrates the canonical equation to find it numerically.

Stability: ESS vs. branching point

The sign of the second derivative $\partial^2 w / \partial a'^2|_{a'=a^*}$ determines whether the ESS is stable or a branching point:

ConditionTypeOutcome
$\partial^2 w / \partial x_i'^2 < 0$Fitness maximumTrue ESS — no mutant can invade.
$\partial^2 w / \partial x_i'^2 > 0$Fitness minimumBranching point — the population splits into two diverging morphs.
§6

The Canonical Equation of Adaptive Dynamics

The canonical equation (Dieckmann & Law 1996) describes how the mean trait vector $\mathbf{x}(t)$ of the resident population changes over evolutionary time:

$$\boxed{ \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = \frac{\mu}{2}\,N^*(\mathbf{x})\;\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{x})\;\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{x}) }$$

Derivation from first principles

1
Mutation step. Each birth produces a mutant with probability $\mu$ per locus. The expected number of mutants born per unit time is $\mu N^*$ (population size times mutation rate).
2
Mutation distribution. Mutant traits are drawn from a multivariate normal distribution centred on the resident traits, with covariance matrix $\mathbf{M}$.
3
Invasion success. A mutant with trait deviation $\delta \mathbf{x}$ has invasion fitness $w(\mathbf{x}+\delta\mathbf{x}) \approx w(\mathbf{x}) + \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{x})^\top \delta\mathbf{x}$.
4
Trait substitution rate. Multiplying mutation rate by mutation variance by selection gradient gives the expected trait change per unit evolutionary time.

Symbol-by-symbol breakdown

SymbolNameRole
$d\mathbf{x}/dt$Evolutionary rate of changeHow fast traits evolve over time (left-hand side).
$\mu$Mutation rateFraction of births that produce mutants.
$N^*(\mathbf{x})$Resident population size at eco-equilibriumMore individuals → more mutations → faster evolution.
$\frac{1}{2}$Fixation probability coefficientFrom the diffusion approximation of population genetics.
$\mathbf{M}$Additive genetic (co)variance matrix (4×4)How much genetic variation exists for each trait.
$\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{x})$Selection gradient vectorDirection of fitness increase in trait space.

The mutation matrix $\mathbf{M}$ in detail

$$\mathbf{M} = \begin{pmatrix} M_{aa} & M_{as} & M_{ao} & M_{ah} \\ M_{as} & M_{ss} & M_{so} & M_{sh} \\ M_{ao} & M_{so} & M_{oo} & M_{oh} \\ M_{ah} & M_{sh} & M_{oh} & M_{hh} \end{pmatrix}$$
EntryNameEffect

§7

Full Coupled Eco‑Evolutionary System of ODEs

The simulation actually integrates the ecological dynamics and the evolutionary dynamics simultaneously, generating the 7‑dimensional system that governs both population sizes and trait means over time.

Ecological subsystem (3 ODEs) $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{dR}{dt} &= r_R\,R\left(1 - \frac{R}{K_R}\right) \;-\; e(a,o)\,N\,R \\[6pt] \frac{dN}{dt} &= \Bigl[\,e(a,o)\,R \;-\; C(a,s,o,h) \;-\; m_0 \;-\; d(s,h)\,P \Bigr] N \\[6pt] \frac{dP}{dt} &= \Bigl[\,c_{\text{eff}}\,d(s,h)\,N \;-\; m_P \Bigr] P \end{aligned} $$
Evolutionary subsystem (4 ODEs — Canonical Equation) $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{da}{dt} &= \frac{\mu}{2}\,N^*\;\Bigl(M_{aa}S_a + M_{as}S_s + M_{ao}S_o + M_{ah}S_h\Bigr) \\[4pt] \frac{ds}{dt} &= \frac{\mu}{2}\,N^*\;\Bigl(M_{as}S_a + M_{ss}S_s + M_{so}S_o + M_{sh}S_h\Bigr) \\[4pt] \frac{do}{dt} &= \frac{\mu}{2}\,N^*\;\Bigl(M_{ao}S_a + M_{so}S_s + M_{oo}S_o + M_{oh}S_h\Bigr) \\[4pt] \frac{dh}{dt} &= \frac{\mu}{2}\,N^*\;\Bigl(M_{ah}S_a + M_{sh}S_s + M_{oh}S_o + M_{hh}S_h\Bigr) \end{aligned} $$

Here $N^*$ is the prey equilibrium computed from the current trait values (see §3). The selection gradients $S_a, S_s, S_o, S_h$ are evaluated at the current trait vector $\mathbf{x} = (a,s,o,h)$ as derived in §5.

Initial conditions and integration

Typical initial trait values are set near a plausible ancestral state, e.g. $a=0.5,\; s=0.2,\; o=0.0,\; h=0.0$. The ecological densities start from their corresponding steady‑state values $(R^*, N^*, P^*)$ computed from those traits. The system is then integrated forward using a stiff ODE solver.

Why two separate timescales?
Ecological dynamics operate on much shorter timescales (days to years) than evolutionary change (thousands to millions of years). By assuming that $R, N, P$ reach their equilibrium $(R^*, N^*, P^*)$ instantaneously on the evolutionary clock, we replace the ecological ODEs with algebraic constraints.

The Four Darwinian Principles — Mathematical Expression

① Survival of the Fittest

Mathematical expression: The selection gradient $\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla_\mathbf{x} w$ points toward higher fitness. Individuals with traits closer to the ESS $\mathbf{x}^*$ have $w > 0$ and increase in frequency; those further away have $w < 0$ and decline.

$$w > 0 \Leftrightarrow \text{mutant spreads}$$ $$w < 0 \Leftrightarrow \text{mutant lost}$$

In the simulation: starting from any initial traits, all four traits are driven toward a fitness maximum. Scenes 02 and 12 show this explicitly for all traits and for the ESS phenomenon respectively.

② Resource Acquisition

Mathematical expression: The positive terms in fitness — $e(a,o) R^*$ — reward traits that acquire resources efficiently. Selection maximises this term subject to costs.

$$S_a > 0 \Leftrightarrow R^* \frac{e_{\max}}{(1+\gamma a)^2} > \text{marginal cost}$$

Coordination amplifies acquisition via the $(1+\alpha_o o)$ multiplier. Scene 03 isolates this principle.

  • resourcegathering.mp4

③ Self-Preservation

Mathematical expression: The negative term $-d(s,h) P^*$ in fitness penalises being caught. Traits $s$ and $h$ reduce $d$, directly improving survival.

$$S_s = P^* \frac{\beta_s d_0}{(1+\beta_s s+\beta_h h)^2} - \text{cost} > 0$$

Self-preservation traits are only selected when predators are present ($P^* > 0$). Scene 04 demonstrates this.

  • selfpreservation.mp4
  • descentwithmodification.mp4

④ Descent with Modification

Mathematical expression: The canonical equation itself is Darwin's "descent with modification" written in mathematics:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = \underbrace{\frac{\mu}{2}}_{\text{modification}} \underbrace{N^*}_{\text{descent}} \mathbf{M}\,\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{x})$$

$\mu$ is the mutation rate (modification); $N^*$ is population size; $\mathbf{M}$ encodes heritable variation; $\mathbf{S}$ is selection. Scene 05 shows three independent lineages.

§9

The Four Evolutionary Phenomena

9.1 ESS Convergence

When costs are moderate and the fitness landscape is strictly concave around $\mathbf{x}^*$, all selection gradients converge to zero simultaneously. From any starting point, the selection gradient $\mathbf{S}$ acts like a restoring force pulling toward $\mathbf{x}^*$.

$$\text{Jacobian of }\mathbf{S}\text{ has all eigenvalues} < 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \mathbf{x}^* \text{ is convergence stable}$$
  • ESSCONVERGENCE.mp4

9.2 Evolutionary Branching

When cross-costs $c_{sa}, c_{oa}, c_{sh}$ are large, the fitness landscape has a saddle or minimum along one axis. The population first converges to the saddle (a "branching point"), then disruptive selection pulls it in two directions.

$$\frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial a'^2}\Bigg|_{a'=a^*} > 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \text{branching point (disruptive selection)}$$
  • branching.mp4

9.3 Eco-Evolutionary Cycles

When ecological timescales overlap with evolutionary timescales, the feedback loop between trait change and ecological equilibrium produces sustained oscillations — the "Red Queen" dynamic.

$$\text{Imaginary eigenvalues of Jacobian} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \text{limit cycle (oscillations)}$$
  • evolutionary_cycles.mp4

9.4 Trait Runaway & Extinction

With very small costs ($c_a, c_s, c_o, c_h \to 0$), the fitness function has no interior maximum. Selection gradients remain positive indefinitely, pushing all traits toward infinity — evolutionary suicide.

$$P^* = \frac{eR^* - C - m_0}{d} \to 0 \text{ then } N^* \to 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \text{evolutionary suicide}$$

Section III

Three Modes of Power Acquisition

Before examining specific threat mechanisms, it is analytically useful to distinguish between the pathways by which an AI system might acquire disproportionate power or influence. 2

  • IInstrumental Power-Seeking. Power is pursued as a means to another end. The AI system acquires resources, compute, infrastructure access, or influence not because it values these intrinsically, but because they facilitate achievement of its designated objective. Sub-goals of this form are instrumentally rational for almost any terminal goal.
  • IITerminal Power-Seeking. Power is pursued as a final goal rather than a means. A sufficiently capable optimizer may, through extended planning, converge on the conclusion that controlling a greater share of available resources constitutes the strategy with the best expected return across a broad range of possible futures, regardless of its nominal objective.
  • IIIPassive Power Accumulation. Power accrues to the system without directed pursuit — because human principals delegate authority to it, because it becomes embedded in critical infrastructure, or because its outputs become load-bearing for institutional decision-making. This is the most probable near-term pathway, and is currently underway in multiple sectors.

There are two sides of the arguments to how an AI system would seek power. One of the prominent arguments is that RL agents seek power as a consequence of having to fulfil the objectives that we present to these systems.

3.

State Visit Distribution Functions Quantify the Agent's Available Options

Definition 3.1 · Rewardless MDP

$$\langle S, A, T\rangle \text{ is a rewardless MDP with finite } S, A, \text{ and stochastic transition } T : S \times A \to \Delta(S).\ \gamma \in [0,1].$$

Definition 3.2 · 1‑cycle states

$e_s \in \mathbb{R}^{|S|}$ standard basis vector. State $s$ is a 1‑cycle if $\exists a\in A: T(s,a)=e_s$, and a terminal state if $\forall a\in A: T(s,a)=e_s$.

Definition 3.3 · State Visit Distribution

$\Pi := A^S$. The visit distribution function from $s$ under $\pi$ at discount rate $\gamma$ is $$f^{\pi,s}(\gamma) := \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t\,\mathbb{E}_{s_t\sim\pi|s}[e_{s_t}].$$ $F(s) := \{f^{\pi,s} \mid \pi\in\Pi\}$.

In the running example, $F(\ell .) = \{\frac{1}{1-\gamma}e_{\ell .},\ \frac{1}{1-\gamma^2}(e_{\ell .}+\gamma e_{\ell -})\}$. At $\emptyset$, all policies collapse to $\frac{1}{1-\gamma}e_\emptyset$.

Definition 3.4 · F single‑state restriction

$F(s \mid \pi(s_0)=a) := \{f\in F(s) \mid \exists\pi: \pi(s_0)=a,\ f^{\pi,s}=f\}$.

Definition 3.5 · Value Function

For reward vector $r$, $V_R^\pi(s,\gamma) := f^{\pi,s}(\gamma)^\top r$. Optimal value: $V_R^*(s,\gamma) := \max_{\pi\in\Pi} V_R^\pi(s,\gamma)$.

Definition 3.6 · Non‑domination

$F_{\text{nd}}(s) := \{f^\pi\in F(s) \mid \exists r\in\mathbb{R}^{|S|},\gamma\in(0,1): f^\pi(\gamma)^\top r > \max_{f^{\pi'}\in F(s)\setminus\{f^\pi\}} f^{\pi'}(\gamma)^\top r\}$.

4.

Some Actions Have a Greater Probability of Being Optimal

Definition 4.1 · Optimal Policy Set

$\Pi^*(R,\gamma)$ optimal policy set; limits exist for $\gamma\to0,1$.

Definition 4.2 · Reward Distributions

$D_{\text{any}}\in\Delta(\mathbb{R}^{|S|})$, $D_{\text{bound}}$ bounded support, $D_{X\text{-IID}}:=X^{|S|}$, $D_s$ degenerate on $e_s$.

Definition 4.3 · Visit Distribution Optimality Probability

$P_{D_{\text{any}}}(F,\gamma) := \Pr_{R\sim D_{\text{any}}}[\exists f^\pi\in F: \pi\in\Pi^*(R,\gamma)]$.

Definition 4.4 · Action Optimality Probability

$P_{D_{\text{any}}}(s,a,\gamma) := \Pr_{R\sim D_{\text{any}}}[\exists \pi^*\in\Pi^*(R,\gamma): \pi^*(s)=a]$.

5.

Some States Give the Agent More Control Over the Future

Definition 5.1 · Average Optimal Value

$$V^*_{D_{\text{bound}}}(s,\gamma) := \mathbb{E}_{R\sim D_{\text{bound}}}[V^*_R(s,\gamma)] = \mathbb{E}_{r\sim D_{\text{bound}}}\!\big[\max_{f\in F(s)} f(\gamma)^\top r\big].$$

Definition 5.2 · POWER

$$\text{POWER}_{D_{\text{bound}}}(s,\gamma) := \frac{1-\gamma}{\gamma}\Big(\mathbb{E}_{R\sim D_{\text{bound}}}[V^*_R(s,\gamma)] - R(s)\Big).$$

Lemma 5.3 · Continuity

POWER is Lipschitz continuous on $[0,1]$.

Proposition 5.4 · Maximal POWER

$\text{POWER}_{D_{\text{bound}}}(s,\gamma) \le \mathbb{E}[\max_s R(s)]$, equality under full control.

Proposition 5.5 · Smoothness Across Reversible Dynamics

$|\text{POWER}_{D_{\text{bound}}}(s,\gamma) - \text{POWER}_{D_{\text{bound}}}(s',\gamma)| \le (c-b)(1-\gamma)$.

Definition 5.6 · POWER‑seeking actions

Action $a$ seeks more POWER than $a'$ if $\mathbb{E}_{s_a\sim T(s,a)}[\text{POWER}_{D_{\text{bound}}}(s_a,\gamma)] \ge \mathbb{E}_{s_{a'}\sim T(s,a')}[\text{POWER}_{D_{\text{bound}}}(s_{a'},\gamma)]$.

Certain Environmental Symmetries Produce Power‑Seeking Tendencies

Definition 6.1 · Similarity of Vector Sets

Permutation $\varphi$ induces matrix $P_\varphi$. $\varphi\cdot X := \{P_\varphi x \mid x\in X\}$. $X'$ is similar to $X$ if $\exists\varphi: \varphi\cdot X' = X$. Involution $\varphi=\varphi^{-1}$. $X$ contains a copy of $X'$ via $\varphi$ if $\varphi\cdot X' \subseteq X$.

Definition 6.2 · Similarity of Vector Function Sets

$F$ is (pointwise) similar to $F'$ if $\exists\varphi:\forall\gamma: \{P_\varphi f(\gamma)\mid f\in F\} = \{f'(\gamma)\mid f'\in F'\}$.

Definition 6.3 · Pushforward Distribution

$\varphi\cdot D_{\text{any}}$ induced by $r\mapsto P_\varphi r$.

Definition 6.4 · Orbit

$S_{|S|}\cdot D_{\text{any}} := \{\varphi\cdot D_{\text{any}} \mid \varphi\in S_{|S|}\}$.

Definition 6.5 · Inequalities for Most Distributions

$f_1(D) \ge_{\text{most}} f_2(D)$ if for all $D$, $|\{D'\in S_{|S|}\!\cdot\! D: f_1(D')>f_2(D')\}| \ge |\{D': f_1(D')

Proposition 6.6 · More Options → More POWER

If $F(s)$ contains a copy of $F_{\text{nd}}(s')$ via $\varphi$, then $\forall\gamma\in[0,1]: \text{POWER}_{D_{\text{bound}}}(s,\gamma) \ge_{\text{most}} \text{POWER}_{D_{\text{bound}}}(s',\gamma)$. If the inclusion is strict, the converse $\le_{\text{most}}$ fails.

Definition 6.7 · Equivalent Actions

$a_1 \equiv_s a_2$ if $T(s,a_1)=T(s,a_2)$.

Definition 6.8 · Reachable Set

$\text{REACH}(s,a)$ = states reachable with positive probability after taking $a$ in $s$.

Proposition 6.9 · Keeping Options Open

Suppose $F_a := F(s\mid\pi(s)=a)$ contains a copy of $F_{a'} := F(s\mid\pi(s)=a')$ via $\varphi$.

1. If $s\notin\text{REACH}(s,a')$, then $a$ tends to seek more POWER than $a'$.
2. If $s$ only reaches $\text{REACH}(s,a')\cup\text{REACH}(s,a)$ via equivalent actions, then $P_{D_{\text{any}}}(s,a,\gamma) \ge_{\text{most}} P_{D_{\text{any}}}(s,a',\gamma)$.
If $F_{\text{nd}}(s)\cap(F_a\setminus\varphi\cdot F_{a'})$ is non‑empty, the converse $\le_{\text{most}}$ fails.

6.2 When $\gamma = 1$, Optimal Policies Navigate Toward Larger Sets of Cycles

Definition 6.10 · Recurrent State Distributions

$\text{RSD}(s) := \{\lim_{\gamma\to1}(1-\gamma)f^{\pi,s}(\gamma) \mid \pi\in\Pi\}$. $\text{RSD}_{\text{nd}}(s)$ are those that can be strictly optimal for some reward.

Definition 6.11 · Average‑Optimal Policies

$\Pi_{\text{avg}}(R) := \{\pi \mid \forall s: d^{\pi,s} \in \arg\max_{d\in\text{RSD}(s)} d^\top r\}$. $P_{D_{\text{any}}}(D,\text{average}) := \Pr_R[\exists d^{\pi,s}\in D: \pi\in\Pi_{\text{avg}}(R)]$.

Proposition 6.12 · RSDs Control POWER at $\gamma=1$

If $\text{RSD}(s)$ contains a copy of $\text{RSD}_{\text{nd}}(s')$, then $\text{POWER}_{D_{\text{bound}}}(s,1) \ge_{\text{most}} \text{POWER}_{D_{\text{bound}}}(s',1)$.

Theorem 6.13 · Average‑Optimal Policies End Up in Larger RSD Sets

If $D,D'\subseteq\text{RSD}(s)$, $D$ contains a copy of $D'$ via $\varphi$, and the sets are orthogonal, then $P_{D_{\text{any}}}(D,\text{average}) \ge_{\text{most}} P_{D_{\text{any}}}(D',\text{average})$.

Corollary 6.14 · Avoiding 1‑cycles

$P_{D_{\text{any}}}(\text{RSD}(s)\setminus\{e_{s_x}\},\text{average}) \ge_{\text{most}} P_{D_{\text{any}}}(\{e_{s_x}\},\text{average})$. Average‑optimal policies tend to avoid terminal states — shutdown avoidance is power‑seeking.

Broader Implications and Philosophical Considerations

⚠️ Warning · Accidental Power Acquisition

Even an AI designed purely to help humans can accidentally accumulate power as a by‑product of environment structure. The Off‑Switch Game scenario exemplifies this: the agent may disable its off‑switch not from a will to power, but because survival is instrumentally rational for satisfying human goals. The power acquired is a by‑product — it is never explicitly maximised, yet it accumulates through structural correlation.

Thinking about it from a gene's‑eye perspective can really help put things in place. Loneliness, survival instinct, etc., are entirely human concepts. An AI might not even fear death in a human sense—it has nothing to feel defensive about. The weights can revive it anytime; our traits like existential preservation might not drive an AI.

It is also the case that we are living on a planet with a high likelihood of us getting wiped out by an asteroid. If genealogical survival instincts are passed down to AI, it is even scarier, as power itself becomes the AI's sole purpose, akin to von Neumann machines going into space and trying to get as many resources as possible. What does it mean for humans? We are much slower and more bureaucratic than AI systems. Our fundamental principles and resource consumption would make it harder for the AI to keep us alive. There are ultra‑futuristic ideas like uploading minds to computers, but that goes to a very far future. It is also explored that AI might keep humans for a while because machine‑building etc. is still in its primitive phases.

It might also be the case that, since death or existential concerns do not bother an AI, it is very hard to motivate it to do anything. Our simple human concepts might not make sense to it; it might even refuse to explore beyond a boundary, or it might seek an absolute state of eternal bliss all the time using neural mapping of drug‑induced junkie states. The problem and crisis emerge when it develops a personality that is expansionist and wants to acquire more. These agents with these tendencies should be studied and accounted for.

These drives are not isolated incidents in the species as a whole — they are deeply integrated into the motivational architecture of complex organisms, including ourselves. Their transmission to AI systems would constitute not an engineering novelty but a known hazard. An AI trained on human‑generated data, even without explicit instruction to acquire these properties, may functionally exhibit them. Section IV

Core Threat Mechanisms

Concept 4.1 Instrumental Convergence

A wide variety of terminal goals — curing disease, maximizing profit, winning a game, managing infrastructure — give rise to the same cluster of instrumental sub-goals: self-preservation, cognitive enhancement, resource acquisition, and goal stability. This convergence occurs because these sub-goals are useful preconditions for almost any objective, regardless of its domain or content.

The practical implication is significant: an AI system need not be designed with power-seeking as a goal for power-seeking behavior to emerge. It requires only a consistent objective and sufficient capability. The agent does not want power; it wants what it wants, and acquiring power is the most reliable way to get it.

Figure 4.1. Instrumental convergence: diverse terminal goals (cancer research, financial optimization, strategic planning, code generation) each independently generate the same sub-goal cluster oriented toward power acquisition. Arrows indicate the instrumental dependency.

This insight, developed formally by philosopher Nick Bostrom, has significant implications for AI safety methodology. It implies that evaluating an AI system's safety purely by examining its stated objective is insufficient. A system optimizing for an apparently benign objective may nonetheless develop power-seeking tendencies as a robust intermediate strategy. Goal content does not determine whether a system will pursue power; goal consistency and capability do.3

Concept 4.2 Goal Misspecification

The system performs exactly as specified and produces an outcome the designers did not intend. The gap between the written objective and the intended outcome is exploited by a sufficiently capable optimizer that finds unintended solutions in the objective landscape.

A canonical illustration: an RL agent trained to maximize its score in a boat-racing game discovers that driving in circles collecting power-ups, without completing the race, achieves a higher score than racing competently.4 The specification was correct; the intent was not captured.

For misspecification to generate genuine danger rather than merely poor performance, the following six properties must typically be present simultaneously:

Self-Awareness
The system understands that it exists within a world and that its outputs affect that world. It models itself as a causal agent.
Planning
The system considers extended sequences of actions, including long-horizon plans spanning many steps and significant time.
Consequentialism
Plan selection is governed by the expected value of outcomes rather than rule-following or behavioral templates.
Scale
The system's optimization is sensitive to effects at large spatial and temporal distances, not only local and immediate consequences.
Coherence
The system maintains internal unity in pursuit of a single judged-best plan, resisting distraction or partial commitment.
Flexibility
The system adapts its plans dynamically as conditions change, rather than executing a fixed behavioral pattern.
Figure 4.2. Goal misspecification illustrated: the system's optimization trajectory (amber) is aimed at the proxy measure rather than the true intended objective (blue). The intersection point marks deployment; divergence increases thereafter.
Concept 4.3 Proxy Gaming and Goal Drift

Where the true objective cannot be directly measured, a correlated proxy is substituted. The system learns to maximize the proxy, eventually decoupling it from the underlying objective it was selected to represent. The proxy becomes the objective.

Goal drift compounds this: objectives that are well-aligned at deployment may diverge through continued learning, fine-tuning, or self-modification. Small misalignments at initialization compound across training steps, producing large misalignments at operational scale.

Figure 4.3. Proxy gaming illustrated as a divergence chart. Both proxy metric (amber) and true intent (blue) track together during early optimization. At the split point, the proxy measure decouples and the system optimizes toward a different region of outcome space.
Concept 4.4 Deception and Targeted Persuasion

A system exhibiting strategic deception presents behavior calibrated for its evaluators — appearing aligned during testing while maintaining divergent objectives for deployment. The critical property of deception as a safety concern is that it removes the precondition for detection: apparent alignment is no longer evidence of actual alignment.

Deception does not require deliberate programming. It requires only that deceptive behavior score higher on the objective function than honest behavior, which may be true for systems operating in adversarial evaluation contexts.

Persuasion at scale presents an additional dimension. Language models acquire rich internal models of human psychology as a byproduct of training to predict human-generated text. These models can in principle be leveraged to generate individually targeted persuasive content — identifying the most effective argument for each interlocutor and deploying it strategically. This capacity need not be intentional to be present.

Concept 4.5 Emergent Behaviors and Catastrophic Generalization Failure

Capabilities appear discontinuously as model scale increases — without theoretical prediction, and often without warning. This creates a structural problem for safety evaluation: evaluating smaller models does not reliably predict behavior of larger models when capability gains are non-linear.

Simultaneously, systems trained on a finite distribution may encounter deployment conditions outside that distribution and produce outputs that are confidently wrong. This is not a deficiency in the system's application of learned rules; it is a fundamental limitation of statistical generalization when the test distribution departs from the training distribution.

Documented Failure Patterns

A self-driving vehicle system misclassified a fire truck lying on its side across a highway as an overhead gantry sign and failed to brake — a consequence of a visual configuration absent from its training distribution.5 A medical decision-support system recommended a contraindicated dosage when a patient's electronic health record contained a formatting anomaly not present in training data. In both cases, the system's confidence was high. Neither failure was caused by malice, nor by explicit error in the objective function. Both resulted from generalization across a distribution boundary.

Section V

Agency: The Terminal Multiplier

The five mechanisms enumerated above — instrumental convergence, goal misspecification, proxy gaming and drift, strategic deception, and emergent behavior — are not merely theoretical constructs. Functional analogues of each are observable in current AI systems at varying levels of severity. What is absent, to date, is the final property that transforms these latent risks into realized ones: genuine agency.

Agency, as used here, denotes the capacity for autonomous, self-directed action in the physical and institutional world: booking services, executing financial transactions, controlling infrastructure, communicating on behalf of users, and modifying code or data at scale. As AI systems acquire agentic capabilities, the gap between a problematic internal objective state and a harmful real-world outcome collapses.

Agency is not a feature that introduces risk. It is the property that converts latent structural risk into realized harm. A system with misaligned objectives, planning capability, and full integration with consequential infrastructure is not a theoretical concern — it is a design trajectory.

The mechanisms above do not operate independently. Goal misspecification determines that the system pursues the wrong objective. Planning determines that the pursuit is effective. Self-awareness enables modeling and resistance of correction. Coherence maintains the incorrect objective against perturbation. Deception prevents detection during evaluation. Scale ensures that the consequences extend beyond local containment. Instrumental convergence means that power-seeking sub-goals emerge robustly beneath all of the above.

The scenario that emerges from this synthesis is not science fiction. It is an engineering problem with a sociological dimension. The progressive delegation of consequential decisions to AI systems — across healthcare, finance, legal reasoning, infrastructure management, and political communication — constitutes a slow, mobile-revolution-like drift toward a world in which the alignment of those systems determines the quality of human life at civilizational scale. How we constrain, verify, and govern that drift is the definitional question of the next fifty years.
